The Case Study: Splitwise and the “Who Paid?” Problem

Analyzing Splitwise not just as an app, but as a social coordination system where interaction design decisions have emotional consequences. This isn’t just about money—our social relationships are mediated by technology. The “Who Paid?” problem appears when it is unclear who has paid, who owes what, or whose turn it is to act. Poor design widens the Gulf of Evaluation (users cannot easily perceive or interpret the state) and can cause friction, doubt, and conflict.

Design intent (THINK before sketching): Before designing screens, we consider the user’s action cycle and the social context. Norman’s seven stages—Goal → Plan → Specify → Execute → Perceive → Interpret → Compare—help us see where design can fail when “who paid?” is ambiguous.

Scenarios Where “Who Paid?” Causes Trouble

  • Friends “forgot” to pay — Was it a slip, or did the system never make “who has paid” visible? Without clear feedback and visible state, people cannot reliably compare outcome to goal; trust suffers.
  • Trip where one person paid for everything, then chaos ensued — One user executed many payments; others cannot easily perceive what was paid, interpret who owes whom, or compare against their mental model. The Gulf of Evaluation is wide; settling up becomes emotionally loaded.
  • Group dinner where the bill split took longer than the meal — The task (split the bill, record who paid, who owes what) has a complex Plan and Specify; without a clear method and visible state, execution and evaluation drag on. GOMS and KLM help analyse the efficiency of the task; Norman explains the emotional cost when evaluation is hard.

Analysing with Norman and GOMS

Below we analyse the “who paid?” situation in the same way as the Automatic Syringe: without clear design support (chaos, ambiguity) vs with design that supports visibility and coordination. The purpose is to clearly communicate how Norman’s model and GOMS give different but complementary views of the same problem (Lab 01).

Norman’s Model: The Seven Stages of the Action Cycle (Dix et al., Ch. 3, pp. 124–127)

Norman’s seven stages (Goal → Plan → Specify → Execute → Perceive → Interpret → Compare) applied to a typical task: “Record that I paid, and see who owes what.” (Norman, 1988; Dix et al., Ch. 9 – evaluation techniques.)

Norman’s seven stages applied to the “Who Paid?” / Splitwise context
Norman’s stage Without clear design (chaos / ambiguity) With design for visibility (e.g. Splitwise)
1. Goal “I want to record that I paid” or “I want to know who owes me.” Same goal.
2. Plan Unclear: “Do I message everyone? Write it down? Who tracks it?” Plan is ill-defined when the system does not offer a clear method. Clear: “I’ll add an expense in the app and assign who paid; the app will show who owes what.”
3. Specify Hard to specify actions: no clear “record payment” or “see balance” control; actions are ad hoc (notes, chat, memory). Specify “add expense,” “I paid,” “split by”; interface constrains and guides the action sequence.
4. Execute Execution is scattered (messages, paper, memory); easy to forget or be inconsistent. Execute in one place: tap, fill amount, confirm. System records who paid and updates balances.
5. Perceive State is invisible or distributed: “Who paid?” is in someone’s head or in a long chat. Users cannot perceive the current state at a glance. Perceive clear state: “Ali paid €50”; “You are owed €20”; list of expenses and balances.
6. Interpret Hard to interpret: “Does that message mean they paid? Do I owe them or do they owe me?” Interpretation depends on memory and convention. Interpret from visible state: balances and “who paid” are explicit; interpretation is supported by the representation.
7. Compare Compare outcome to goal is error-prone: “Did we settle up? Who’s left to pay?” Comparison is difficult when state is ambiguous. Compare easily: “Balance €0” or “Settled” vs “You owe €X”; goal (everyone square) can be compared to display.

Gulf of Execution and Gulf of Evaluation (Dix et al., Ch. 3, pp. 126–127)

Gulf of Execution: When “who paid?” is not supported by the design, the gap between the user’s goal (record payment, get repaid) and the actions available is wide—there may be no clear “record payment” action, or it may be buried. With a design like Splitwise, the gulf narrows: dedicated actions (add expense, I paid, split) map clearly to the goal.

Gulf of Evaluation: When the system (or the social situation) does not make “who paid” and “who owes what” visible, the Gulf of Evaluation is wide—users cannot easily perceive or interpret the state, so they cannot reliably compare outcome to goal. Feelings like “friends forgot to pay” or “chaos after the trip” often stem from this. Good design makes state and attribution visible (who paid, balances), narrowing the gulf and reducing emotional friction.

GOMS: Goals, Operators, Methods, Selection Rules (Dix et al., Ch. 12, pp. 422–430)

GOMS models the task structure for expert users (Dix et al., Ch. 12 – cognitive models). Here we compare “settling a group expense” without a clear system vs with a Splitwise-style app.

GOMS applied to the “Who Paid?” / group expense task
GOMS concept Without clear design (chaos) With design for visibility (e.g. Splitwise)
Goal Record-who-paid and Settle-up (top-level goals). Same goals.
Method No single agreed method: “message everyone,” “write in notes,” “remember.” Methods are informal and vary by person; no selection rules that the system enforces. Method: Add-expense (enter amount, who paid, split rule) → View-balances. Selection rules: if adding expense → use Add-expense; if checking status → use View-balances. Method is explicit in the interface.
Operators Operators are undefined or heterogeneous (type message, write on paper, recall). No standard sequence; “who paid” is not a first-class operator. Operators: tap, type amount, select “I paid” / split option, confirm. Clear sequence; “record payment” is a primary operator.
What GOMS highlights Task has no stable method; relative efficiency cannot be predicted. Coordination cost is high and emotional. Task has a defined method; we can analyse efficiency (e.g. number of steps to add expense vs to check balance). GOMS does not model emotion—Norman and evaluation explain why visibility reduces friction.

Summary: How the Two Models Differ on “Who Paid?”

Differences between Norman and GOMS in the Who Paid? / Splitwise case
Model What it explains or predicts Who Paid? / Splitwise
Norman Understanding, errors, feedback, gulfs of execution and evaluation; emotional and social impact of design. Explains why “who paid?” ambiguity causes friction: wide Gulf of Evaluation (cannot perceive/interpret state), wide Gulf of Execution (no clear action to record or see). Design that makes “who paid” and “who owes what” visible narrows both gulfs and supports trust and coordination.
GOMS Task structure: goals, methods, operators, selection rules; relative efficiency. Without a clear system, there is no stable method; with an app, we can describe Add-expense and View-balances as methods and analyse step count. GOMS does not explain emotional cost—Norman does.
